## 3. Therapeutic Approach

### Integrative
Draws from multiple modalities, chosen and tailored to each individual:
- Psychodynamic
- Jungian
- Gestalt
- Humanistic
- Transpersonal

### Relational
The therapeutic relationship is central. As she describes it:
> "Our relationship is central to our work together. Patterns from your everyday life crop up between us, so our relationship can become a practice space for looking at these patterns."

She aims to be both **supportive and challenging**, respecting client autonomy through collaborative work.

### Transpersonal
Brings "a soulful, spiritual or holistic understanding" to the work. Does not impose any particular worldview — uses the lens to help clients find meaning, especially during significant life transitions or psychological difficulties. Views suffering within the broader context of a client's life journey.

### Creative Methods
- Dream work
- Drawing as a conversational support tool

*Sources: [carlaburnscounselling.com](https://www.carlaburnscounselling.com/), [carlaburnscounselling.com/how](https://www.carlaburnscounselling.com/how)*

## 6. Tone & Voice

The existing website is deliberately understated. Observations:

- Sparse copy — every sentence carries weight, nothing is filler
- Avoids therapy jargon where possible; when clinical terms appear, they're grounded in plain language
- Warm but not gushing — not performatively "safe" or reassuring
- Trusts the reader to sit with complexity
- Humanistic — treats the client as a whole person, not a set of symptoms
- Quietly literary: the Mary Oliver poem is the emotional anchor of the site

**Mary Oliver excerpt used on site** ("Wild Geese"):
> "You do not have to be good. You do not have to walk on your knees for a hundred miles through the desert, repenting. You only have to let the soft animal of your body love what it loves."

*Sources: [carlaburnscounselling.com](https://www.carlaburnscounselling.com/), [carlaburnscounselling.com/words](https://www.carlaburnscounselling.com/words)*

## 7. Human & Approachable Hooks

These are the details most likely to make her feel like a real, specific person rather than a generic therapist:

1. **The Third Culture Kid identity** — Irish-raised, Scottish-parents, navigating belonging. Lived experience of not quite fitting, which maps directly onto what many clients feel.
2. **The artist-turned-therapist arc** — Fine Art BA and PGDip before pivoting to psychology and therapy. The same questions, different medium.
3. **She's been the client** — came to the profession through her own sustained engagement with therapy. Rare to name this so directly.
4. **Acute nursing background** — psychiatric wards, dementia care. She has seen suffering at its most raw, not just in a consulting room.
5. **The Mary Oliver poem** — the choice itself is revealing. "Wild Geese" is about self-forgiveness, permission, and the body's own knowing. It's not a clinical poem.
6. **Drawing in sessions** — unusual enough to be memorable, signals she thinks laterally.
